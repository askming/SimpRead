# This Map Ranks Places In The Bay Area By Economic Diversity

The Bay Area has become a land of ballooning wealth and staggering contrasts, where gilded [billionaires](https://archive.ph/o/PpM38/https://www.sfchronicle.com/realestate/article/off-market-billionaires-row-21095504.php) live just a mile or two away from some of the nation’s most visible [poverty](https://archive.ph/o/PpM38/https://www.sfchronicle.com/vault/portalsofthepast/article/Once-fashionable-S-F-Tenderloin-s-spectacular-17557711.php). Meanwhile, once-mixed enclaves like [Palo Alto](https://archive.ph/o/PpM38/https://www.vox.com/podcasts/23604908/malcolm-harris-palo-alto-capitalism-interview) have become uniformly rich, as housing demand pushed working class families [ever further](https://archive.ph/o/PpM38/https://www.sfchronicle.com/bayarea/article/longest-commute-contra-costa-county-20168678.php) from major job centers.That prompted a question: Are there any places in the Bay Area where the haves still live side-by-side with the have-nots? The question isn’t purely academic: Researchers find that the [quality of life](https://archive.ph/o/PpM38/https://www.macfound.org/media/files/hhm_brief_-_mixed-income_neighborhoods_expand_social_networks_benefit_health.pdf) for everyone tends to be better in mixed neighborhoods.AdvertisementTo see whether that kind of mixture still existed, the Chronicle analyzed income data for the Bay Area’s roughly 1800 census tracts — each a mini-neighborhood generally consisting of several thousand people. Using that income data, we calculated a statistical measure called “[entropy](https://archive.ph/o/PpM38/https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_(information_theory))” to figure out how mixed each neighborhood was in terms of the share of households that fell in five buckets of income: Up to $50K, $50-100K, $100-150K, $150-200K and $200K or more. The analysis is based on data collected by the Census from 2020 to 2024.The results of that analysis show that even in the 2020s, there are plenty of Bay Area neighborhoods that are surprisingly mixed.

Chronicle • Source: U.S. CensusOne drawback to this analysis is that the available income buckets aren’t ideal: Census data doesn’t let us break down the $200k-plus households more finely — and that group now makes up a third of all the households in the region.Still, a few remarkable trends emerge. For one thing, the less-densely populated areas, often in the hills, ringing the major cities of the East Bay and South Bay generally have minimal income diversity. That’s because they’re rich: Most of the households in places like Saratoga, Orinda and Woodside easily clear the $200,000 income threshold.That’s also increasingly true for less ritzy places like Fremont and Mountain View, where [good schools](https://archive.ph/o/PpM38/https://www.sfchronicle.com/bayarea/article/fremont-best-place-19507398.php), nice houses and proximity to tech job centers have made the population almost uniformly high-earning.Other places lack income diversity for the opposite reason. While the Bay Area has nothing like the racial (and economic) segregation of many [eastern metropolises](https://archive.ph/o/PpM38/https://www.statista.com/chart/27566/major-us-metro-areas-with-highest-black-white-neighborhood-segregation/), parts of West Oakland and East Oakland stand out as persistently low-income enclaves, as do Chinatown, the Tenderloin, and parts of the Bayview in San Francisco.But some areas of the Bay remain — or have become — places where working-class families live close to much higher-earning ones.
### Neighborhoods become mixed for many reasons

All things being equal, scholars say economically mixed neighborhoods are [better](https://archive.ph/o/PpM38/https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/27116/412292-Effects-from-Living-in-Mixed-Income-Communities-for-Low-Income-Families.PDF) than ones where everyone is on the same footing.George Galster, a retired urban economist at Wayne State University, says low-income residents benefit from living among the rich, who are better able to demand better city services and amenities. “But it also benefits higher-income folks as well," he said. "Imagine a world… where all low income people live on the fringes of a city and have to commute huge distances. That hurts the quality of the workforce, the air, and so forth.”But those benefits depend heavily on how the neighborhood got to be mixed — and whether it stays that way.“I would make two important distinctions: Is a place stably mixed or temporarily mixed?” said Galster.“Temporary” mixing usually means one thing: gentrification. If a neighborhood in transformation looks diverse, “maybe you’ve caught it at a moment where it’s moving from one income group to another,” said Galster.That seems to be the case in East Palo Alto. Once known for its high [crime rate](https://archive.ph/o/PpM38/https://www.ktvu.com/news/east-palo-alto-records-zero-homicides-2025) and [poverty](https://archive.ph/o/PpM38/https://www.smcgov.org/district-4/history-east-palo-alto%23:~:text=Following%20incorporation,%20East%20Palo%20Alto,and%20law%20enforcement,%20perpetuating%20violence.), the city is now one of the most mixed communities in the Bay Area, as families looking for bargains on the formidably expensive Peninsula [decamp](https://archive.ph/o/PpM38/https://thecampanile.org/30633/spotlight/the-price-of-progress/) there. The same is increasingly true of much of Contra Costa County, where former factory workers in towns like [Pittsburg](https://archive.ph/o/PpM38/https://www.sfchronicle.com/bayarea/article/pittsburg-california-steel-mill-21307691.php) mix with wealthier newcomers.

Outside the North Bay, the pattern tends to be the opposite: Urban areas have the most mixture, and suburbs have become monochromatically wealthy.
### The most mixed neighborhoods represent different slices of Bay Area life

Some of the most mixed Census tracts are urban. For example, Adams Point in Oakland is the most mixed area in the East Bay, whose households are just about equally distributed across the five income buckets. That neighborhood is moderately ethnically diverse too: Just over half of residents are white, with the remainder about evenly split between Black, Hispanic, Asian, and mixed-race residents. It’s also unusually young, with an average age around 35.

#### About the analysis

To perform this analysis, the Chronicle collected income data on Bay Area census tracts for the 2020-2024 5-year vintage of the American Community Survey (table B19001). That data source breaks tract-level incomes down into 19 buckets, ranging from $0 to $10,000 all the way up to $200,000 or more.The analytic measure selected for measuring diversity across these groups was [Shannon entropy,](https://archive.ph/o/PpM38/https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_(information_theory)) which measures how evenly (or unevenly) observations are distributed across a series of buckets. A higher value denotes a more even distribution.While granular, those 19 buckets were not, in practice, suitable for assessing income diversity in the Bay Area, where fully a third of households have incomes greater than $200,000, and relatively few have incomes below $100,000. The Census enumerates far more income groups at the lower end of the household income spectrum; an entropy analysis across the 19 buckets therefore found that extremely poor tracts had the highest apparent level of diversity. For this reason, the Chronicle decided on five larger groupings.The Chronicle automated some of the data processing with an AI tool, ChatGPT Pro, but checked the results manually.![logo](https://archive.ph/PpM38/6167cae0a6d9d67324d47a205fbefc79fb82f8be.png)
